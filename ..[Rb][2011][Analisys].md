## 3. Human Activity (Climbing) and Its Potential Impact

The report explores ‚Äî cautiously ‚Äî a possible correlation between climbing activity and reproductive failure:

**Suggestive evidence:**
- The South face has 19 climbing routes + 1 via ferrata and a 90% failure rate, the worst of all faces.

**Contradictory evidence:**
- The West face has only 1 via ferrata yet also shows 80% failure ‚Äî suggesting other factors are at work.
- The East face has 9 routes but only 24% failure. The report attributes this to the **large extension** of the East face and the **concentration of routes in one specific sector**, leaving most nesting areas undisturbed.

This is perhaps the most alarming finding in the report:

The South face decline alone is stark: from 26 pairs in 1999 to just 11 in 2011, a **57.7% reduction** in that face alone. ## 6. Key Interpretive Tensions and Limitations

The report is scientifically honest about what can and cannot be concluded:

**Microclimate vs. disturbance:** The South and West faces are naturally hotter and more exposed ‚Äî conditions known to increase egg and chick thermal stress. This confounds any attempt to isolate climbing as a cause of failure. The near-identical failure rates on the West (80%, 1 route) and South (90%, 20 routes) suggest **climatic stress may be the dominant driver** on sun-exposed faces.

**Methodological constraint:** Three visits are insufficient. The recommended protocol (Del Moral, 2009) calls for 8‚Äì10 visits every 15 days from January through May. Earlier visits would detect initial occupation and early abandonment that this study missed.

**Sample size:** With only 4 orientations as analytical units, no valid statistical test can be performed on the climbing-failure relationship.

### 1. The Most Directly Relevant Study ‚Äî Bearded Vulture & Human Activities

**Arroyo, B. & Razin, M. (2006).** "Effect of human activities on bearded vulture behaviour and breeding success in the French Pyrenees." *Biological Conservation*, 128(2): 276‚Äì284.

üîó [ScienceDirect (abstract)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S000632070500399X) | [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Effect-of-human-activities-on-bearded-vulture-and-Arroyo-Razin/8eff029526f1c58c5df00444253cf3d282944795)

This suggests that single-season failure rates may undercount the true cumulative impact.

This paper is directly cited in the Pe√±a Rueba 2011 report as "Gonz√°lez et al. 2006" via a ResearchGate summary. It is the most methodologically rigorous study linking frequency of human passage near nests to measurable reproductive failure in a European vulture.

## Key Synthesis for the Pe√±a Rueba Context

The literature collectively points to several mechanisms that could be at work at Pe√±a Rueba simultaneously:

**Proximity and frequency** matter more than mere presence. The Arroyo & Razin (2006) threshold of <500‚Äì700 m for pedestrian disturbance, combined with Zuberogoitia et al.'s (2008) alert distance of ~307 m, suggests that South-face routes distributed across the entire wall could keep adult vultures off nests repeatedly throughout the critical incubation and early chick-rearing windows.

**Cliff aspect is a confounding but also interacting variable.** The Covy et al. (2019) result ‚Äî that East-facing cliffs independently support the highest avian diversity and abundance ‚Äî mirrors the Pe√±a Rueba data almost exactly, and underlines the point the 2011 report made honestly: you cannot statistically separate orientation effects from disturbance effects without controlling for one or the other.

**Cumulative failure has carry-over effects.** If the Arroyo & Razin (2006) finding applies (that pairs that fail due to disturbance are more likely to skip the following year), then the dramatic colony decline from 121 pairs in 2008 to ~55 in 2011 may partly reflect an accumulating multi-year consequence of disturbance-driven failures, not just a single poor season.
